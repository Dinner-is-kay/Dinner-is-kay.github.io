## Career overview: Historian

A historian is a professional who studies, interprets, and presents the past through research, writing, and teaching. Historians often work in academia, museums, archives, government agencies, or public history institutions. Their work involves analyzing primary and secondary sources, constructing arguments, and communicating findings through books, articles, lectures, or exhibits.
The daily work of a historian varies depending on specialization but typically includes archival research, reading historical texts, analyzing documents, and writing. Historians may also teach courses, curate museum exhibits, or contribute to public discussions about history and its relevance today. I've always been interested in history, stemming back to my childhood when visiting many museums and historic places, and this helped spark a deep and abiding love for history in my heart. As I grew older and went through the school system, I developed my love for history as I learned about more and more different time periods, places, and ideas, and as I went to university, I knew there was only one thing I really wanted to major in. For someone studying history, this career is appealing because it allows for deep intellectual engagement, the opportunity to contribute new knowledge, and the ability to connect past and present in meaningful ways. Historians need many skills in order to remain effective in their work, including critical reading and analytical thinking, strong writing and argumentation, research methodology and source evaluation, attention to detail, ability to synthesize large amounts of information, and communication skills for both academic and general audiences.

With the advent of LLMs entering the public sphere in a manner never before seen, they have created challenges for many industries. Many people have been fired across the job market either directly or indirectly due to AI in some form or fashion, and the historian profession has certianly not been unaffected by this recent development, particularly in the research and writing areas. One major area of disruption is archival research. AI tools can rapidly scan, digitize, and analyze large collections of documents, reducing the need for manual searching. Optical character recognition (OCR) and machine learning models can transcribe handwritten texts, making previously inaccessible sources searchable and further reducing the need for people in the historian field like translators. Another area is writing and synthesis. AI also can generate summaries of secondary literature, suggest interpretations, and even produce draft historical narratives. This could reduce demand for some traditional research roles or change expectations for productivity. AI may also impact teaching, as automated tools can generate lesson plans, grade assignments, and provide tutoring. This could shift the role of historians in education from content delivery to facilitation and interpretation. Finally, AI introduces challenges in authenticity and originality. If AI can generate convincing historical writing, it may become harder to distinguish between original scholarship and machine-assisted work. All these have combined to reduce human reliance in many parts of the historian field where previously the only way to fufill them was with a human.
